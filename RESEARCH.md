# Equations-as-Prose Conventions and the Evolution of Sainani/Thorne/Wheeler Writing Principles: A Research Brief for an AI Manuscript-Review Skill

## TL;DR
- **Topic 1 verdict:** Thorne's 1987 rule that displayed equations are punctuated as part of the prose is still the universal, explicitly stated house style at every major physics/astronomy journal checked — Physical Review/APS, MNRAS, the AAS journals (ApJ/AJ), and (via IOP/copyediting practice) Classical and Quantum Gravity. The companion rules (don't start a sentence with a symbol, don't use symbols as verbs, punctuate multi-line displays on the final line) are equally current and are codified in independent authorities (AIP/AAS style guides, Knuth's *Mathematical Writing*, and Higham's *Handbook*, 3rd ed. 2020). Build these as hard checks.
- **Topic 2 verdict:** Sainani has published *no* peer-reviewed papers on writing pedagogy; her course restates an established canon (Zinsser's "clutter," Williams' *Style*, Strunk & White, Gopen & Swan 1990, Schimel 2012). The principles are validated indirectly (jargon reduces citations; readability is declining) but the specific "cut clutter" advice is only mixed-to-weakly supported empirically — and at least one large study found the most common brevity/clarity advice is associated with *fewer* citations. There is also a substantial scholarly counter-tradition (Hyland on hedging/metadiscourse) showing some "clutter" does real epistemic work. A review skill should treat these as heuristics with confidence levels, not absolute rules.
- **For the skill design:** Separate "hard" typographic/grammatical conventions (high precision, journal-backed, safe to autofix-suggest) from "soft" stylistic heuristics (clutter, voice, terminology) that need context-awareness and an allowlist for legitimate hedging and field-specific jargon.

## Key Findings

### Topic 1 — Equations as prose

1. **The punctuation rule is universal and explicit.** APS ("Punctuate mathematical expressions and displayed equations as part of the sentence"), MNRAS ("Equations should be punctuated as part of the sentence"), AAS/ApJ ("Equations are read as part of the flow of a sentence and are punctuated as such"), and the Physical Review Style and Notation Guide ("Even though displayed math is separated by space from the running text it still is a part of that text and needs to be punctuated accordingly") all state it directly. This has *not* evolved away from Thorne 1987 — it has hardened into formal house style.

2. **"Don't begin a sentence with a symbol"** is in the Physical Review guide ("Avoid beginning a sentence with a symbol...") and Thorne tip #5; AAS phrases the related rule that equations should not be referred to by number alone ("say 'substituting in equation (45)' rather than 'substituting in (45)'") and "Equation (1)" is spelled out at the start of a sentence.

3. **"Don't use math symbols as verbs"** is Thorne tip #4 ("do not write 'When the sky is blue, A=B'") and is echoed in math-writing guides (the "=" sign should not be used outside a formula / as a verb in running text).

4. **Multi-line environments** (align, gather, multline): punctuation goes at the logical end of the complete equation (the last line), and the equation is punctuated as if the whole display were a single grammatical unit. Practical guides recommend the "[DISPLAY] placeholder" test: replace the display with a placeholder, punctuate the sentence, and the terminal mark (comma/period) attaches to the final line. Number on the last line for multi-line displays.

5. **Authoritative references** beyond the journals: the *Physical Review Style and Notation Guide* (APS); the AAS Style Guide (Chandrasekhar-derived); the AIP Style Manual; Knuth, Larrabee & Roberts, *Mathematical Writing* (MAA, 1989, from Stanford CS209); and Nicholas J. Higham, *Handbook of Writing for the Mathematical Sciences*, 3rd ed. (SIAM, 2020) — the standard modern reference, with a chapter on mathematical writing covering punctuation of expressions and equation breaking, plus a LaTeX chapter.

### Topic 2 — Sainani/Thorne/Wheeler principle updates

6. **Sainani has no peer-reviewed writing-pedagogy publications.** Her authority rests on the MOOC "Writing in the Sciences" (Stanford; lectures CC-BY) and her science-journalism background. Her peer-reviewed record is in statistics (the "Statistically Speaking" column in *PM&R* since 2009; she is that journal's statistical editor). There is no updated peer-reviewed codification of her writing rules.

7. **The "clutter" framing is Zinsser's**, not Sainani's — William Zinsser's *On Writing Well* has a famous chapter "Clutter." The "consistency / don't use elegant variation" principle (the "Banana Rule") traces to Fowler's *The King's English* (1906, "elegant variation," defined as "all substitutions of one word for another for the sake of variety") and the journalistic "elongated yellow fruit" trope (Charles W. Morton, *The Atlantic*); in science writing it is stated by Schimel (2012) and Budker (2006). The "banana" mnemonic itself is not original to Sainani.

8. **Empirical status is mixed.** Jargon is shown to reduce citations — Martínez & Mammola (2021, *Proc. R. Soc. B* 288(1948):20202581), titled "Specialized terminology reduces the number of citations of scientific papers," found that "as the proportion of jargon words in the title and abstract increased, the number of citations decreased." Plavén-Sigray, Matheson, Schiffler & Thompson (2017, *eLife* 6:e27725), analyzing a corpus of exactly 709,577 abstracts published 1881–2015 from 123 scientific journals, concluded: "we show that the readability of science is steadily decreasing. Our analyses show that this trend is indicative of a growing use of general scientific jargon." Both support anti-jargon/consistency advice but are correlational. Crucially, the "cut clutter / keep it short" rule was tested directly by Weinberger, Evans & Allesina (2015, *PLOS Comp Biol* 11(4):e1004205), who found: "Surprisingly, half of the typical suggestions—including those that are most common, about brevity and clarity—are associated with a significant decrease" in citations (corpus of >1 million abstracts across eight disciplines, 17-year span).

9. **There is a scholarly counter-tradition.** Ken Hyland's body of work (*Hedging in Scientific Research Articles*, 1998; "Talking to the Academy," Written Communication 1996; metadiscourse work through 2017) shows hedges and metadiscourse perform essential epistemic and interpersonal functions — i.e., not all "clutter" is dead weight. A naive clutter-cutter risks stripping legitimate hedging. There are also published defenses of the passive voice in medical/methods writing.

10. **AI-assisted writing review already exists in this exact space.** The `labarba/sciwrite` GitHub "Agent Skill" encodes Sainani's methodology into five sequential audit passes — clutter extraction; voice/verb vitality (passive, nominalizations); plus terminology consistency, structure, and numerical/citation consistency checks — across four review modes (full, single-section, targeted, interactive). It is CC-BY and explicitly does not alter scientific content. This is the closest existing prior art to the user's planned skill.

## Details

### How the major physics/astronomy journals handle equations as prose

**Physical Review / APS.** The APS "Style Basics" pages (shared across PRL and the Physical Review journals) state: "Neatly format, punctuate, and align equations to bring out their structure. Number equations on the right. Punctuate mathematical expressions and displayed equations as part of the sentence." The longer *Physical Review Style and Notation Guide* adds the rationale and the begin-with-a-symbol rule: "Punctuate mathematical expressions as sentences or parts of sentences" and "Avoid beginning a sentence with a symbol if the sentence before it has ended with a symbol or number." It also gives worked examples with terminal commas on displayed equations followed by "and the reduced transition temperature t is defined to be..." — i.e., commas/periods are part of the displayed math.

**MNRAS.** The author guidelines and the `mnras` LaTeX guide both state: "Equations should be punctuated as part of the sentence. Displayed equations are ranged left (i.e. no indent)." The MNRAS template even shows it in code: the quadratic-formula example ends with a period inside the `equation` environment (`...{2a}.`). MNRAS additionally uses left-aligned (fleqn) equations and requires all displayed equations to be numbered.

**AAS journals (ApJ, AJ, ApJS, ApJL, PSJ).** The Manuscript Preparation page states: "Equations are read as part of the flow of a sentence and are punctuated as such." It also gives the citation convention ("substituting in equation (45)" not "substituting in (45)") and requires all displayed equations to be numbered, with appendix equations as (A1), (A79), etc. The reinstated AAS Style Guide (Chandrasekhar/Hopkins lineage) governs notation consistency.

**Classical and Quantum Gravity (IOP).** IOP's author guidance is lighter on the punctuation-of-equations point in the public-facing pages (it focuses on equation preparation: editable text, solidus for small fractions, italic variables, `exp` for powers of e, consecutive numbering, display long equations separately). However, IOP house copyediting follows the same convention as the wider physics literature, and arXiv-posted IOP/physics papers show terminal punctuation on displays ("Punctuate equations with commas or periods when they are part of a sentence"). Treat CQG as following the universal convention, applied at the copyedit stage rather than spelled out for authors.

**Convergence point:** Across APS, MNRAS, AAS, and IOP practice the rule is identical and unchanged since Thorne wrote it in 1987. The only cross-journal *differences* are mechanical (MNRAS left-aligns and mandates numbering of every equation; APS/AAS number only important/referenced equations; AAS/MNRAS use author-date references while PRL uses bracketed numerals).

### Thorne 1987 and what has and hasn't changed

Thorne's five-page "Some Specific Tips About Technical Writing" (Caltech, 28 July 1987; still hosted on his Caltech page, "Most of those tips are as useful today as when he wrote them") states the relevant rules in Section IV (Equations):
- Tip 3: "In most journals and books equations are treated as part of the prose, and must be punctuated as part of the prose (with commas, semicolons, or periods at the ends of the equations)."
- Tip 4: "Mathematical symbols normally should not be used as verbs; e.g. do not write 'When the sky is blue, A=B.' Instead write 'When the sky is blue, A is equal to B.'"
- Tip 5: "Sentences normally should not begin with mathematical symbols."
- Tip 6: "Do not use built-up equations in the middle of text; any built-up equations should be set off as display equations."
- Tip 7: equation breaking — make a "careful and wise decision about where it is to be broken."
- Tip 10: "Give an equation number to every equation that any reader might ever have any desire to refer to."

All of these survive intact in current journal style. The document is prescriptive and informal (written for his own students), and explicitly hedges with "normally" — useful precedent for a skill that flags rather than mandates.

### Knuth and Higham — the cross-disciplinary anchors

Knuth, Larrabee & Roberts, *Mathematical Writing* (MAA Notes #14, 1989), from Knuth's 1987 Stanford CS209 course, gives the same rules in a math-CS register: equations are part of sentences; punctuate them; don't use "=" as a verb outside a formula; the text preceding a display often needs no special punctuation. (Knuth's example #23 notes that text preceding displayed equations need not use special punctuation — a subtlety a naive checker could get wrong.)

Higham's *Handbook of Writing for the Mathematical Sciences* (SIAM; 1st ed. 1993, 2nd 1998, 3rd 2020) is the standard modern reference. Its mathematical-writing chapter explicitly covers "how to punctuate mathematical expressions and how to break equations across lines"; the "Dos and Don'ts of Mathematical Writing" section includes "Punctuating Expressions — Mathematical expressions are part of the sentence and so should be punctuated" and "Otiose Symbols — Do not use mathematical symbols unless they serve a purpose." The 3rd edition (2020) adds chapters on Workflow, indexing, refereeing, and expanded LaTeX/English-usage material.

### Multi-line equation punctuation — the practical rule

For `align`, `gather`, `multline`, `split`: the display is one grammatical unit, so the terminal punctuation attaches to the last line, and the equation number (if any) goes on the last line. The widely taught technique (e.g., University of Illinois math LaTeX tips) is: "A good way to get the punctuation right is to replace the display by a place holder, such as '[DISPLAY]' and punctuate accordingly." If the surrounding sentence is "Since f is monotonic, we have [DISPLAY], where...", then no punctuation precedes the display and a comma terminates it. `multline` is logically a single equation (single tag, on the last/first line). For lists of consecutive equations presented as a list, separate them with commas and a terminal period, and put "where..." clauses after a comma.

### Topic 2 — the principles, their lineage, and their evidence

**Provenance of the rules.** The "cut clutter" framing is William Zinsser's (*On Writing Well*, chapter "Clutter"). "Consistency over elegant variation" is Fowler & Fowler, *The King's English* (1906), Chapter III, defining elegant variation as "all substitutions of one word for another for the sake of variety"; the journalistic "elongated yellow fruit" trope (Charles W. Morton, *The Atlantic*) is the popular illustration. In science writing the principle is stated by Joshua Schimel, *Writing Science* (OUP, 2012) and bluntly by Dmitry Budker ("Some rules of good scientific writing," arXiv physics/0608246, 2006): "if one is writing a scientific paper, using different words for the same object could be a disaster." Sainani's course explicitly teaches "Recognize that it is OK to repeat key words in scientific writing" — the same idea. Gopen & Swan's "The Science of Scientific Writing" (American Scientist, 1990) is the foundational reader-expectation paper underlying much of this canon (subject-verb proximity, stress position, topic position).

**Modern guides that build on Thorne/Wheeler.** Schimel's *Writing Science* (2012) reframes the whole paper as story structure (OCAR/ABDCE arcs, opening-challenge-action-resolution). Stephen Heard's *The Scientist's Guide to Writing* (2nd ed. 2022, Princeton) is the more process-oriented companion; both explicitly "recommend following principles over rules." Higham (2020) is the math-sciences analogue. These are the live, widely cited successors; there is no single canonical "Thorne 2.0."

**Validation/critique.** Supporting evidence is largely indirect and correlational: Martínez & Mammola (2021, *Proc. R. Soc. B*) found that "as the proportion of jargon words in the title and abstract increased, the number of citations decreased"; Plavén-Sigray et al. (2017, *eLife*, 709,577 abstracts) found readability "steadily decreasing," driven by "general scientific jargon." But Weinberger, Evans & Allesina (2015, *PLOS Comp Biol*) tested concision-type rules against citation data and found that "half of the typical suggestions—including those that are most common, about brevity and clarity—are associated with a significant decrease" in citations. A 2025 parallel-corpus study found readability formulas were "unrelated to comprehension or retention" (though verb-heavy text eased comprehension, supporting the "strong verbs" rule). The strongest *critique* is Ken Hyland's hedging/metadiscourse research, which shows that hedges and metadiscourse are functional, not filler — a direct caution against over-aggressive clutter removal.

**Existing AI prior art.** The `labarba/sciwrite` Agent Skill (CC-BY; built in a GWU course on Generative AI for Engineering Research) already operationalizes Sainani's methodology: five passes (clutter extraction; voice/verb vitality; terminology/keyword consistency; structure; numerical/citation consistency), four modes (full, single-section, targeted, interactive), structured severity-tagged output, and a stated guardrail that it "does not alter scientific content, data, or technical claims." It explicitly recommends giving the tool a full section (not isolated sentences) so it can detect cross-paragraph patterns, and warns about field-specific writing conventions as an edge case.

## Recommendations

**Stage 1 — Build the hard, journal-backed checks first (high precision, low false-positive risk):**
1. **Equation terminal punctuation.** Flag displayed equations (`equation`, `align`, `gather`, `multline`, `eqnarray`, `\[ \]`) whose surrounding sentence structure implies a missing terminal comma/period. Use the placeholder-substitution logic. For multi-line environments, check only the *last* line for terminal punctuation and the number placement.
2. **Sentence-initial symbol.** Flag sentences beginning with a math symbol or "(n)" equation reference; suggest "Equation (n)" or "The quantity X...".
3. **Symbol-as-verb.** Flag "=", "<", ">", "∝" used as the main verb in running prose (e.g., "the energy = ...").
4. **Built-up math in running text.** Flag inline fractions/integrals that should be displayed.
5. **Unnumbered referenced equations / numbering placement.** Journal-configurable (MNRAS = number everything; APS/AAS = number important ones).
*Threshold to change approach:* if false-positive rate on the punctuation check exceeds ~10% on a test corpus, downgrade from "suggest fix" to "flag for review," because the placeholder logic is sensitive to parser errors around "where..." clauses and Knuth's "no punctuation before the display" cases.

**Stage 2 — Build the soft heuristics with calibrated confidence and allowlists:**
6. **Clutter/concision and voice/verbs:** implement but tag as suggestions, not errors. Maintain a **hedging allowlist** (may, suggest, likely, consistent with, etc.) informed by Hyland's work so the tool does not strip epistemically necessary hedges — especially in Discussion/limitations sections. Permit passive voice in Methods.
7. **Terminological consistency ("Banana Rule"):** detect when the same referent is named by varying terms; this is high-value and well-grounded, but requires entity/synonym detection — flag candidate inconsistencies for human confirmation rather than auto-rewriting.
8. **Make checks journal-aware:** ship style profiles for APS, MNRAS, AAS, IOP/CQG (left-align vs centered; number-all vs number-important; author-date vs bracketed-numeral references; British vs American spelling for MNRAS "Acknowledgements").

**Stage 3 — Differentiate from prior art and add value:**
9. Study `labarba/sciwrite` directly (it is CC-BY and reusable) and extend it where it is weakest: it is general-purpose and Sainani-derived; your physics/astronomy angle (equation-as-prose typography, journal-specific math style) is a genuine gap it does not cover. Add a math-typography pass grounded in the APS/AAS/MNRAS guides, Knuth, and Higham.
10. Cite the canon in the skill's documentation (Thorne 1987; Knuth et al. 1989; Higham 2020; Gopen & Swan 1990; Zinsser; Schimel 2012; Hyland 1998) so users can see provenance and confidence level for each rule.

## Caveats
- **Correlational evidence.** The jargon→citations and readability-decline findings are observational, not controlled comprehension experiments; readability formulas (Flesch, Dale-Chall) have been shown not to track actual comprehension. Present "cut clutter" as a well-supported heuristic, not a proven causal lever — and note the Weinberger et al. (2015) finding that the most common brevity/clarity advice was associated with *fewer* citations.
- **Hedging is not always clutter.** Hyland's research is the key reason a clutter-cutter needs domain awareness; aggressive concision can damage the epistemic calibration of scientific claims.
- **CQG/IOP punctuation rule is inferred** from general physics copyediting practice and arXiv examples rather than an explicit author-facing statement; verify against a recent typeset CQG article before hard-coding.
- **No "Thorne 2.0."** There is no single authoritative modern update to Thorne's tips; the field has diversified into Schimel, Heard, Higham, and Gopen rather than converging on one successor document.
- **Sainani's "banana" wording** is associated with her course but the underlying principle predates her (Fowler 1906; Zinsser on clutter); credit the lineage rather than attributing origination.
- **Source quality note:** several full texts of the Physical Review Style Guide and Higham's Handbook were accessed via secondary hosts (studylib, docplayer, silo.pub) rather than the publisher; the substantive quotes were cross-checked against the official APS and SIAM pages where possible. The Martínez & Mammola sample size could not be independently re-confirmed from the primary source and is therefore not stated as a precise figure here.
